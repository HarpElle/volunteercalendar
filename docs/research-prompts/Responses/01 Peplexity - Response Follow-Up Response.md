# Based on what you just told me, what's the single most important thing I'm NOT asking about that I should be?

The biggest missing piece is **volunteer retention and experience economics**—i.e., modeling churn, burnout, and replacement cost of volunteers as a first‑class product constraint, not just a UX nicety.

## Why this matters more than almost anything else

- ChurchTrac’s data and guidance emphasize that **scheduling itself is a primary driver of church volunteer burnout**, and that poor handling of availability, rotations, and last‑minute changes pushes volunteers to quit roles they otherwise value.[^1][^2]
- Volunteer‑retention research in church contexts finds that **intrinsic motivation, social connection, and how volunteers are treated by leaders explain over 50% of the variance in retention**, far more than demographic factors, meaning systems that make people feel overused, confused, or unseen will materially shrink the volunteer pool.[^3][^4]
- Industry content for church scheduling tools frames automation (fair rotations, self‑scheduling, automated reminders, easy swaps) explicitly as a way to **prevent burnout and keep volunteers long‑term**, not just to “save admin time,” because losing a trained volunteer creates downstream costs in recruitment, onboarding, and reduced ministry capacity.[^5][^6]


## What you should be asking explicitly

- “How do we **measure and improve volunteer retention** inside VolunteerCal (e.g., serving‑frequency scores, burnout‑risk alerts, satisfaction signals)?”[^1][^3]
- “How should our **scheduling engine be constrained by retention heuristics** (max frequency per role, mandated rest weeks, fair load balancing, self‑scheduling options) rather than only by coverage?”[^2][^7][^1]
- “What does it cost a 200‑person church, in lost capacity and staff time, when a core volunteer quits—and how can our product **demonstrate ROI primarily via reduced volunteer churn**, not just ‘hours saved’?”[^8][^3]

If you treat “healthy, sustainable volunteer teams” as the core optimization target—and build metrics, algorithms, and UI around that—you get differentiation that existing ChMS and scheduling tools mostly talk about in blogs but do not operationalize deeply in product.
